Scheduled Tribes Education Poverty Employment Policy Analysis

When Schooling Does Not Become Security

A data-driven policy analysis of Scheduled Tribe education, poverty, employment, dropout, and livelihood distress across high-ST states in India.

Yuvraj Verma DSM Final Project April 2026 Policy Data Analysis

Introduction

This project studies the relationship between Scheduled Tribe educational outcomes and broader livelihood indicators across Indian states. The analysis focuses especially on high-ST states, where education, poverty, employment, and social disadvantage often overlap in complex ways.

Instead of only asking whether schooling levels are high or low, the project asks whether education outcomes are translating into better livelihood security. This makes the analysis useful for identifying where policy support may be most urgently needed.

Problem Statement

The main problem addressed in this project is that better educational access does not always lead to better economic outcomes for Scheduled Tribe populations. States may show improvement in enrolment or literacy, but still face high dropout, poverty, weak employment outcomes, or dependence on public employment schemes.

Therefore, the project asks:

How do educational outcomes among Scheduled Tribe populations relate to employment, poverty, and livelihood distress across high-ST states in India?

Analysis and Results

Q1. How do ST literacy and literacy gaps describe long-term educational exclusion?

The correlations between ST literacy rate and employment or poverty outcomes are weak and noisy. The scatter of literacy rate against work participation shows a loose positive trend, but with enough deviation that literacy rate alone explains very little of the variation in WPR across states. Similarly, the literacy gap against WPR runs in the expected direction — larger gaps coincide with lower work participation — but again the relationship is too scattered to treat as causal. What this tells us is not that literacy causes employment or poverty in a direct, measurable way, but rather that both literacy and livelihood outcomes are downstream of the same long-run structural conditions: historical access to schooling, geographic integration, and administrative reach over decades. Literacy is measuring accumulated exclusion, not a current lever. The literacy gap adds a genuinely distinct layer — it flags whether women were left behind even within already-disadvantaged ST communities — which the aggregate literacy rate obscures entirely. The two indicators together describe the shape of historical exclusion, but they are poor predictors of near-term economic outcomes, and they should not be confused with the current schooling pipeline, which needs GER and dropout to be assessed properly.

Q2. Do states with stronger ST schooling participation, measured by GER, also have lower dropout and lower ST poverty?

Q3. Are enrolment and GER enough to indicate educational progress, or do dropout rates tell a different story?

The near-zero correlation between GER IX–XII and secondary dropout is the central finding here, and it is important to read this carefully without inferring causation in either direction. High GER does not cause dropout, nor does it prevent it — they are measuring fundamentally different things. GER captures whether children are being enrolled at the right age relative to the school-age population; dropout captures whether enrolled students are staying through secondary completion. A state can inflate GER through enrolment drives, conditional cash transfers, or age-relaxed admissions without building any of the retention infrastructure — school quality, proximity, teacher attendance, household economic security — that actually keeps students in school. The high-GER-but-high-dropout states in the notebook's sub-group table are the clearest evidence of this: they are not contradictions but natural consequences of treating access and retention as the same problem when they are not. The high-GER-but-low-WPR states add another layer — even completion without labour market integration does not produce the livelihood outcomes that the education investment is supposed to generate. GER is a politically legible indicator and an administratively easy one to report, which is precisely why it tends to dominate policy tracking. The dropout rate at secondary level is harder to collect and less flattering to report, but it is a closer approximation of whether the schooling system is actually working.

Q4. Is the literacy gap more informative than ST literacy alone in explaining disadvantage?

Q5. Are there states where educational indicators look reasonable, but employment or poverty outcomes remain weak?

The mismatch classification is a descriptive exercise — states are assigned flags based on median thresholds, not on any underlying causal model — but the pattern it reveals is substantively important. States like Assam, Nagaland, Arunachal Pradesh, and Tripura appear with multiple simultaneous livelihood distress flags despite having education indicators that are not the weakest in the sample. The temptation is to read this as evidence that education investment in these states has "failed," but that inference is not supported by the data. What the mismatch more plausibly reflects is that the constraints on livelihood outcomes in these states are operating primarily on the demand side of the labour market: thin formal employment sectors, geographic isolation, dependence on subsistence agriculture, and weak integration with broader economic networks. Education can improve a person's readiness to participate in a formal economy, but it cannot create that economy where it does not exist. The mismatch states are therefore not evidence against education investment; they are evidence that education investment without complementary economic development produces credentialed but underemployed or informally employed populations. Policy designed purely around school access or even dropout reduction will not resolve the livelihood distress visible in these states.

Q6. Does MGNREG unmet demand reflect deeper livelihood distress among ST households?

Q7. Are scholarship-supported states seeing lower secondary dropout among ST students?

The absence of a negative correlation between per-capita scholarship release and secondary dropout is easy to misread as evidence that scholarships do not work. That inference is not valid. The notebook's own framing flags that scholarships are likely targeted toward higher-need, higher-dropout states, which would mechanically produce a positive or flat association between funding and dropout even if scholarships are genuinely helping at the margin within those states. This is a classic confounding-by-targeting problem: the treatment is concentrated where the problem is worst, so cross-sectional correlations will not recover the treatment effect. What the data can flag is the utilization gap — states where release is relatively high but utilization remains low — which is actionable without requiring causal inference. Low utilization indicates that money is not reaching students, whether because of awareness failures, administrative friction, conditionality barriers, or delayed disbursement. Those states need process reform rather than additional allocations. More broadly, the scholarship findings reinforce that financial support is one necessary input among several; retention also depends on school quality, teacher presence, household opportunity costs, and the perceived value of secondary completion for future employment — none of which scholarship disbursement directly addresses.

Q8. Do states with high ST schooling participation still depend heavily on MGNREG?

Q9. Does gender parity in enrolment translate into better female literacy and work outcomes?

The weak correlation between GPI and female work participation is one of the more important cautionary findings in the notebook. Near-parity enrollment for girls at the secondary level, measured by GPI, does not produce a detectable signal in female WPR across these states. This is not evidence that enrolling girls in school is unimportant — it almost certainly matters for long-run outcomes — but it does mean that the timeline and the intervening mechanisms are too complex to appear as a clean cross-sectional correlation. Several states with GPI values close to one still show low female WPR and large female literacy gaps. The gaps in literacy reflect multi-generational exclusion that current enrollment parity cannot undo quickly. The gaps in work participation reflect labour market norms, mobility constraints, household-level gender expectations, and the structure of available employment — all of which operate largely independently of whether girls are currently enrolled in secondary school. Treating GPI improvement as a sufficient policy signal for gender equity is therefore misleading. It conflates the input (enrollment) with the outcome (economic participation), and it can create the appearance of progress on gender equity while the substantive gaps in literacy and livelihood remain intact.

Q10. Does secondary dropout appear to be a stronger warning signal of poverty and weak labour outcomes than literacy alone?

Q11. Do high-ST-share states systematically perform worse, or do outcomes vary substantially?

The scatters of ST population share against literacy, poverty, and WPR across all states show shallow slopes and high dispersion, meaning that knowing a state's ST concentration tells you very little about where its ST outcomes will land. This is an important corrective to a common heuristic in policy discussions that treats high-ST-share states as a homogeneous problem category. The variation within the high-ST group is real and substantial: states at similar levels of ST concentration differ sharply on literacy, dropout, poverty, and employment. Concentration is not a cause of poor outcomes; it is a demographic characteristic that correlates loosely with historical marginalization but is not the operative mechanism. The implication is that national-level interventions designed for "high-ST states" as a bloc will almost inevitably be poorly calibrated for a large proportion of their intended beneficiaries — over-intervening in states that have made relative progress and under-addressing the specific structural failures in states that have not.

Q12. Do states with high concentrations of ST villages show different education and livelihood outcomes?

Q13. Are there gender-specific disadvantages hidden behind state averages?

The female literacy gap — the difference between male and female ST literacy within each state — is a more diagnostic indicator of gendered exclusion than either aggregate literacy or GPI alone. The bar chart makes this concrete: Rajasthan, Odisha, Jharkhand, Jammu and Kashmir, and Madhya Pradesh carry gaps that in some cases exceed 15 to 20 percentage points within the ST population alone. The scatter of female literacy gap against female WPR shows a negative association — larger gaps tend to coincide with lower female work participation — but the correlation should not be read as the literacy gap causing low WPR. Both are more plausibly outcomes of the same underlying structural conditions: norm systems that have historically deprioritized women's education and economic participation, geographic and social barriers to female mobility, and the absence of formal employment opportunities that would make female literacy economically legible to households. What the data establish is that state-level averages — including overall ST literacy averages — can present a picture of relative progress that completely conceals the depth of female disadvantage within the same population. In states where the gap is large, the policy need is not simply more general ST education spending; it is specifically gendered intervention reaching adult women in the current generation through literacy programmes, and adolescent girls in the next generation through retention, safety, and post-schooling economic pathway support.

Limitations

This analysis is exploratory and should not be interpreted as causal evidence. The dataset combines indicators from multiple sources and years, so some variables may not be perfectly aligned in time.

Some indicators, such as ST poverty, may also have missing values for certain states. Because of this, results should be used for policy interpretation and prioritization rather than precise causal claims.
